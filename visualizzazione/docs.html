  1. Introduction and overview

“Donne Illustri” is a project that aims to develop a system to convert the information stored in a digital book, encoded in epub format,  into a source of knowledge[1]. To do so, we used the technologies developed for the Semantic Web (RDF) to describe data stored in the text and enrich them with data coming from the Linked Open Data environment.

The overall concept of this project is:

  1. Converting the existing text in XML/TEI format;
  2. Expanding its information with LOD;
  3. Visualising the converted text and the enriched knowledge.

It is important to stress that this project does not aim to solve all the issues and aspects that could emerge during the process, but to produce and propose a suitable workflow that could be reused and implemented for future projects.

A second clarification is that this project does not aim to create a structure able to describe all the existing and possible types of book, but it focuses on the development of a process that can be applied to all those monographs used (or potentially used) in education, therefore focusing on scholastic or academic topics.

  1. Theoretical background

The research domain in Scholarly Digital Editions (SDE) has been characterised so far by a strongly document-centred approach. Recently a new paradigm is emerging, that is trying to change this perspective. The idea used in projects like “Il quaderno di appunti di Paolo Bufalini”[1] is an evident effort in the direction of opening the SDE to the Semantic Web and the RDF technologies. The idea is to realise “knowledge graphs'', marking interconnections using the graph data structure. The distance taken by the publishing industry from the SDE has been observed for a long time so far[6], therefore it is not a big surprise that even the Semantic Web and the Linked Open Data (LOD), have little to none interest in this industry.

This project was conceived in this direction: producing a model and a workflow that could show a direction to expand the usage of the Semantic Web and LOD technologies in the publishing industry. Here we do not discuss the effective economic opportunity of such a system, we only propose a possible technical approach for publishing houses to deal with these phenomena.

The idea of this project is based on the theoretical division of readers in readers and users[5]: while the first ones are mostly interested in finding a reliable source of the work, the second ones, mostly focused on studying the text, that look at the intertextual context of the text, its correlations. While Rasmussen applies this to the reader of SDE, we try to apply this parameter also to readers to other sources.

Our project looks to those readers that lie in the middle between these two groups, namely all of those readers, mainly students, that read the text as a source of knowledge. Because of this particular characteristic of them, we consider the most suitable technology for this new category the Semantic Web, since it transforms information into knowledge.

Moreover, as our workflow enhances, we believe that the inclusion of such an effort could be very advantageous for the publishing houses, since it could allow the content of their texts to be exploited in the so-called nanopublications[3], offering a higher visibility to the resources. A nanopublication can be defined as “a set of annotations that refers to the same statement and contains a minimum set of agreed-upon annotations”.

  1. Defining the source

As anticipated in the Introduction, this project focuses on academic and educational material, starting from epub material. Our decision landed on Vite di artiste eccellenti by Costantino D’Orazio. This book explores the lives of many female artists in history, presenting itself as a valid counterpart to the renowned Le vite de' più eccellenti pittori, scultori, e architettori by Giorgio Vasari. This text represent a valid case study for our work:

  • It focuses on an academic topic but not specific for education; because of this, its level of depth is not too high to be hyper specific, and yet not too shallow to be useless.
  • The historical coverage of events is significantly broad, presenting subjects coming from different ages.
  • It indicates different types of works of art, texts, and entities.

Only the central chapters of the resource have been analysed here for reasons that will be further explained.

  1. Modelling the UML

  1. The production of the UML

Any text, book and bibliographic record can be conceptualised around two dimensions, their structure and their content[8]. A first attempt to develop a model of our data tried to take in consideration both this level and a high-depth level (Figure 1). We realised that this approach was too broad, resulting in a general translation of both the ontology FaBiO, which is used for describing bibliographic records, and other ontologies common in the field of Digital Humanities (BIBO, CIDOC/CRM).

A better approach seemed to summarise this first model, focusing on the key aspect of the academic text and its content in the Humanities Figure 2. This graph, way more readable, has two core nodes: fabio:Book and fabio:ScholarlyWork. These two nodes represent respectively the structure of the texts (citations, charts, images, quotes) and the content (subjects, actors, events cited, time-spans).

Considering our source and similar ones, we noticed that, for the aim of our project, it was not significant to focus on the layer represented by fabio:Book, so we reconceptualised and simplified our reflection, reaching the final UML. In this model, we consider the content of a book as a Work, as it would be conceptualised in FRBR; it is important to notice that Work in this specific case represents any kind of textual entity. The core classes that a Work can refer to are Places, Time-spans, Events and, in the Humanities field, Artistic object, and other texts (as the recursive edge emphasises) Figure 3. The Text can cite other Works, while directly refers to the classes that are included in the work. We consider this model to be a good representation of a generalised model of detecting data stored in a general topic based on Humanities. Of course, if dealing with different branches of knowledge, it should be enriched (because of this, here we provide the previous models).

We consider our modelling process compliant with the three key aspects that where proposed by Elena Pierazzo[2]:

  1. We selected our main interest and simplified our model.
  2. This simplification was guided by the point of view that we were adopting towards our domain.
  3. Our model deals with both functions of modelling.

  1. Workflow

This paragraph of our documentation is meant to describe the process that we have followed to develop our initial idea and concretize the modelling.

  1. From ePub to XML/TEI

The digital format preferred by publishing houses is the ePub format. It consists of a collection of HTML or XHTML files stored all together. This multi-document structure did not comply with a characteristic that we wanted for our text, to be ODD (One Document Does it all)[2][8].  Said so, the XML/TEI seemed  a valid alternative since it can contain the whole text in one document. This translation was facilitated since both documents follow a similar SGML syntax. This correction allowed us to preserve some elements of the text that were already marked in the ePub file (headings, italics) and preserve some significant elements, such as the note’s internal references.

  1. Including entity

i.  Defining describing elements

The second step of our process was to mark the entities in the XML file. It must be noticed that this was the most time consuming activity. In similar projects like the Bufalini notes [1], a key advantage in the activity was that the entities were already marked. The publishing house did not include it.

Because of this absence, we had to analyse our UML and trying to convert it in XML/TEI[2]. Luckily, almost all the entities we were interested in are present in the model of P5 Guidelines. We used <person> and <place>.

 A major problem was represented by the codification of the work of art. At the state of the art, there is no such thing as an element to describe works of art. We have decided to create an ad hoc tag <item> to identify our work of art. Infact, our aim was not to use XML/TEI as a model for describing the artistic resource in our text[3]. This decision was possible because of the high freedom that the TEI leaves in the definition of new ad hoc tags[2].

A second problem was the differences in the types of works of art contained in our source text. Describing art history from the origins until current days, the types of artworks that may be encountered can differ dramatically. Because of this, we decide to focus on the three main works of art: painting, architecture and sculpture, defined as type attributes of the element. Marking these entities was particularly difficult since the original text sometimes names the works of art with the title and the year, sometimes it just mentions it not explicitly.

ii.  Identifying entities

As anticipated before, this phase was the most time consuming of all. Since the text was not marked, we had to do it on our own. To accomplish this process we decide to perform two different strategies, with the aim to identify the most efficient one:

  1. Manually marking the text. The logical process is to:
  1. Read the text;
  2. Individuate the entities and create them;
  3. Report them in the TEIHeader.
  1. Semi automatic process:

Facilitated by the spacy NER pipeline this task required only a thorough cleanup and review, the final output was in fact an overly tagged version of the final one, meaning that the algorithm is trained and tuned in order to recognize as many Named Entities as possible, often pushing its chances. This caused some easily recognizable errors, namely capitalised terms at the beginning of the sentence were recognized sometimes as Entities and some random substrings were tagged too. This class of error was the easiest to address, and it made the text, as mentioned before, “overly tagged”, this means that every real Entity that we wanted to tag (People and Places) was already there. The second class of errors, and the most insidious one, was that related to misconceptions between People and Places,when a person is mentioned using just the surname it was difficult for the ML algorithm to distinguish between an actual surname and was sometimes tagged as a place.

In the end we can affirm that this kind of ML tasks under aware human supervision can facilitate and improve the tagging task for digital texts.

                   

The two strategies performed in similar ways as a matter of time (more or less one day per chapter). In both cases we decided to define the entities only by the name, without adding extra information about occupation or gender for People, or coordinates for Places. This decision will be better explained in the following section.

  1. Expanding data with WikiData

Once we had the entities in our TEIHeader and marked them in the body, we proceeded  with the extraction of the information stored for each entity in Wikidata[4]. The decision of using Wikidata is a mere example, and any other similar resource could be used for the same purpose. It is actually strongly suggested to enrich the description in such a way due to some limits that will be analysed further. What we are going to describe in this subparagraph and in the following one can be seen in the GitHub repository in the file rdf.jpynb.

i. Defining WikiData Entities and Properties

The first step for working in WikiData was to convert the UML in Classes and Properties as in Wikidata; this step is fundamental to query the content of WikiData. The result is the one as shown in Figure 4.

While the definition of Work of Art Q838948 and Person Q5 were quite immediate, the retrieving of the Class Geographic location Q2221906 was obtained with a reversed process: we searched for a small set of representative elements of different type of object representing Places (streets, cities, countries, continents) and we queried the superclasses that those objects have.

As the graph above shows, we used Wikidata to retrieve some pieces of information external to those contained in the text, which are:

  1. For People:
  1. Date of birth.
  2. Date of death.
  3. Date of death.
  4. Place of death.
  5. The gender.
  6. The occupation.
  7. Work location (will be better analysed after).
  1. For Places:
  1. Longitude
  2. Latitude
  1. For Works of Art:
  1. Date of creation.
  2. Place of creation.
  3. Place of conservation.
  4. The subject(s).
  5. The genre(s).

It may be interesting to notice that we retrieve some information that is already in the text or could have been injected by it (gender and occupation of artists). As mentioned before, we decided not to include that information in the TEIHeader element representing the entities due to the unstructured nature of data in the text. This decision was driven to streamline the process, however it opens some possible weaknesses.

ii. Querying WikiData

Due to the experimental nature of this project, even in this case we used two different strategies for identifying the entity we were interested in and its data. The first one was used for People and Places; it can be summarised as:

  1. Search for the entities having as label in Italian (the language of our text) the same label contained in the TEIHeader. If not in italian, search it in English
  2. If only one return all the data between those selected that you have.
  3. If none return the label.
  4. If more than one return the label and all the available entities with that label.

The main advantage of this method is its automation in the first part, where the labels are queried by the machine. The main disadvantage is that there are many cases in which the entity may be multiple. In this last case, the human operator will have to check manually all the entities returned to choose the correct one, if any.

This approach has a high significance from a methodological method, since it allows to define which are the entities that the text names that are not in WikiData. As we will stress in the Conclusions, this number shows a significant gap between the knowledge owned by the author and the WikiData dataset.

The second method was used for detecting the works of art produced by the female artists of our collection. For this type of element, the label would not have been a valid alternative due to many issues: many similar labels i.e. self portrait;  high level of discrepancy between the label defined in the text and the one provided by WikiData; the absence of an explicit title in the text. The strategy adopted for accomplish the task can be summarised as follows:

  1. Search manually the titles of the work in WikiData if any, otherwise search for the works in Google;
  2. Insert for those found in WikiData the attribute same as to the element in the TEIHeader;
  3. Extract the sameAs from the TEIHeader and perform the necessary query.

While this first method may seem a bit redundant in the first stages, it seems to be the most efficient one, since using a unique identifier has a better result. Of course this approach is way more prone to human error in identifying the resources.

The results of these queries showed us one of the main problems in modelling data in Digital Humanities. In fact, the birth and death dates, when only the year is known, returns the result as the firs of january, which enphasises how fuzzy, as defined by Elena Pierazzo[2], data can be.  

  1. From WikiData to RDF

i. Why RDF?

It must be pointed out strongly and clearly that starting from this point the implementation of our resource is only a placeholder which behaves somehow like the final version, but it is only for displaying. In fact, as shown by the documentation of the Bufalini’s Notebooks semantic edition to access a knowledge graph in a website developing a server-side web application and managing the RDF data available on the triplestore which would be stored in a Blazegraph database. Developing an infrastructure such as the one just mentioned would require some expertise and competencies that the authors do not possess. We still have decided to dedicate a portion of our workflow to create this RDF.

This decision is driven by the high heterogeneity of data we will process. Moreover, as we will address in the future developments, new type of data and relationships among them may be defined. Finally, using RDF allows the creation of a limited semantic and structured set of classes and properties that will facilitate the retrieval of the information stored in the graph.  

ii. Modelling from Wikidata to other Ontologies

The first fundamental step to follow is to define the ontology that we will use to describe the three different entities that we have defined.

To do so we have translated the model described by Figure 4 in the following model (Figure 5). 

This model uses three ontologies: schema.org[5], CIDOC/CRM[6] and FOAF[7]. The decision of adopting schema.org was moved due to the technical limitations aforementioned. In fact, this ontology allowed us to avoid using similar properties coming from CIDOC/CRM: this ontology is event-based, which means that for each event a new entity event should have been created (Figure 5). This would have resulted in a very verbose RDF graph. This approach is the one used from some existing resources such as LinkedArt[8].

iii. Encoding RDF graph

Once defined the ontologies to use, we defined the operations for  creating the RDF graph. We used the XML/RDF format, which allowed us to store everything in one file, as said before. The coding process can be found inside the same file as that of the queries. The computational approach followed is:

  1. For each entity use the ID in the TEIHeader;
  2. For each value in the result of the query create the respective triple;
  3. If the predicate refers to an entity contained in the TEIHeader, use the TEI ID;

This allowed the creation of an RDF strongly connected to the TEI which would allow the connection between the text and the RDF.

During this face we performed an interesting pruning which is worth mentioning. We decided to include for the artists subject of our text only the working places that are mentioned in the text. To do so, we decided to retrieve all the places mentioned in the chapters where they are mentioned in the heading. It must be stressed that this approach is strictly connected to the structure used in our source text, and it should not be considered as an abstraction strongly reusable.

  1. Including IIIF

  1. Finding IIIF

IIIF is a standard that has been emerging recently in the Digital Humanities community. It was born from the joint efforts of different institutions[9]. Since our work deals with visual items and the original source text does not include a visual reproduction of the images, we thought it would have been interesting using this young technology to see its application in a context as the one we are dealing with. The first objective in this phase is to individuate, if any, the Manifest of any of the works of art we have in our text. Of over sixty one Institutions listed in the IIIF guidelines on how to find a resource, only five are art museums or galleries. Not the most optimistic starting point. Querying all these resources would have been extremely time consuming, luckily there are two of them that can work as aggregators: WIkipedia and Wikidata. Unfortunately the former does not work; likely the latter does. We used the suggested query to retrieve the manifest of a certain author; the query as the one following:

#defaultView:ImageGrid{"hide": ["?image"]}

SELECT ?thingLabel ?image  ?thing (URI(CONCAT('https://tools.wmflabs.org/mirador/?manifest=', STR(?iiif))) AS ?iiiflink) WHERE {

wd:Q120512327 wdt:P6108 ?iiif;

   wdt:P170 ?author;. # creator:

OPTIONAL{ ?thing wdt:P18 ?image }

SERVICE wikibase:label { bd:serviceParam wikibase:language "[AUTO_LANGUAGE],en". }

}

We have searched for each of our authors manually and we only found one of our pictures. This result shows how little is the usage of IIIF in the Museums and Galleries ecosystem (the majority of the items we encountered were drawings, probably stored in one of the institutions listed in the IIIF webpage). As much as analysing the benefits and the disadvantages of using IIIF for museums and galleries would be interesting, it would diverge from the topic of this documentation.

  1. Using IIIF with Mirador

It must be stressed one last time that the content shown here is only a simulation of the final behaviour. As mentioned above, we were able to retrieve only one iiif manifest out of all the works of art that were mentioned in the text. We took the manifest (which is in the form of a JSON file) and decided to use a javascript function to visualise it with Mirador, an open-source, web-based viewer that allows for the exploration and comparison of digital objects. Mirador is a great tool for visualisation because it enables the users to interact with the digital resources, fostering collaborative research and scholarly analysis. We inserted the script in an HTML page and we linked it to the passage of the text in which the painting is mentioned (by doing so we allowed the readers to access the resource through a link and see it firsthand).

  1. CeTEIcean

To visualise the result of our work we used CETEIcean[10], a Javascript library that allows TEI documents to be displayed in a web browser without first transforming them to HTML. It uses the emerging Web Components standards, especially Custom Components[11]. The Custom Elements are a type of component that are created from a script and include all of the resources needed in order for the script to work. This allows Lens Creators to bundle together script and resources in a convenient package.

We have integrated the basic tools of the library with some ad hoc functions. This can be found in the vizualizationName.html. What the functions do is to read the Custom Elements loaded by CeTEIcean and dynamically update the content of the sidebar where the information about the piece of text clicked are dynamically shown.  

This function shows the content of the RDF graph, but it must be pointed out that the selection of this content is based on html element selectors. In fact, what this function does is not query the graph, but search in the copy stored in the TEIHeader (converted by CeTEIcean but hidden) and display the content of the element.

  1. Results

  1. Limits

  1. Technical limits  

As shown in the previous session, our implementation has some strong deficiencies that are evident and have been pointed out throughout this documentation. To summarise them:

  1. The absence of an infrastructure to operate over the RDF graph.
  2. The development of the front-end  based on it.
  3. Dealing with multiple entities in WikiData.
  4. CeTEIcean can result as a double-edged sword: in fact even if this technology is getting more visibility in the online community, it still requires a high level of expertise in javascript in order to be used as we did. This could limitate the reuse and the dissemination of it as a cooperative method in the Digital Humanities environment.
  5. As indicated before, for our research we decided only to expand our data with those found in Wikidata. This infrastructure, even if it can be considered one of the most efficient in the field, still presents some issues, namely:
  1. Many entities are missing.
  2. Does not present, as stated before, a unique class Place, which makes the alignment with XML/TEI elements difficult.
  3. It has been noticed that there are two different properties for indicating the dc:creator.

ii.    Conceptual limits

To the nature of our project, the theoretical and conceptual dimension was more significant than the technical one. Because of this, we want to stress more on the limits of the model we have developed. We will first analyse the limits concerning the specific domain of Art History as the focus of our text, and then on the general conceptual description of a book in an educational environment.

A first problem that might be pointed out is the limitation of our study only to those works of art that are strongly encoded. We missed many types, mostly in the domain of performing arts, which are still debated in the Digital Humanities environment.

Another problem faced in our project is the limited number of classes and entities that have been marked in the XML/TEI text. As said before we wanted to try a semi automatic mark-up system using already existing libraries for parsing a text. This method was already fallacious with a limited number of entities to recognise. To our knowledge, there is no  system nowadays that would retrieve more specific entities. Moreover, it would have been interesting to mark in the text some properties to enrich our knowledge graph.

  1.  Future Developments

Considering the aforementioned limits we encountered in our project, here we can indicate some future developments, we logically divide them in two parts: on one hand these improvements to better implementations of the already developed tools, on the other new tools that could enrich our project.

  1. Improvement of existing tools

A first significant improvement of our XML could be to expand the markup process including new tags for entities (i.e.marking institutions, events) and developing a system to include in it a structure to mark information about relationship between entities (i.e the marriage between two people).

On the LOD side, a better alignment with the CIDOC/CRM entity based model should be pursued. Not only but including also the text mentioned and the cited could open up the published book to a dimension of interconnection similar to the one developed in the open science domain.

Another useful implementation regards the places we tagged in the text. We didn’t manage to include maps in the project , which are a powerful tool in the context of education. A future development could concern the addition of maps related to the lives of the artists and their main work locations, useful for study purposes. Adding these elements could really help students to have a spatial configuration of the book and to give them some instruments to carry out a discussion about the importance of quantitative studies in literary analysis. As Moretti said “What do literary maps do ... First, they are a good way to prepare a text for analysis. You reduce the text to a few elements, and abstract them from the narrative flow, and construct a new, artificial object like the maps that I have been discussing. And with a little luck, these maps will be more than the sum of their parts: they will possess 'emerging' qualities, which were not visible at the lower level. [The map] offers a model of the narrative universe which rearranges its components in a non-trivial way, and may bring some hidden patterns to the surface[7]”.

  1. Development of new tools

Here we describe some possible tools that could be developed based on the realisation of an RDF database operable as in the Bufalini’s Notebooks. A first tool would be creating a “Discover page” where the user could query all the entities separately from the text (as developed in Bufalini’s Notebook website). Moreover, the information displayed in the navbar in the version implemented so far of the text page, could include not only the data, but clickable links to navigate the aforementioned collection.

A graph database queryable could be used to perform some analysis over the text we are dealing with. We don’t think about a specific set of information, but a free possibility to navigate the resource as done in the Polifonia Project[12]. Considering our target audience, it could be an interesting tool to retrieve information and create knowledge in their studying process. For example, dealing with the text we proposed, they could be interested in identifying which are the most common subjects for female artists, or the main cities where they worked. This topic would open up to many possible techniques of data and information visualisation as proposed by different scholars[7][2].

Finally, it could be interesting to present our project in an ePub file and study how to translate the web resource in such a file format. It is interesting to point out that a resource like this is meant, as we just said, mainly for students who would be mostly interested in reading the text and then studying. In such a case the paperback effect provided by the ePub format on an eReader device could feel more familiar to the user, more than a web resource. A possible solution, that we just leave as an idea, could be to use for the display in a plain format like the one of the ePub, a solution like the one we proposed here, where all resources are not linked but only data are shown. The web application could be inserted in it.

5.  Conclusions

The "Donne Illustri" project aims to convert data from digital books, encoded in epub format, into a source of knowledge by utilising Semantic Web technologies (RDF) to enrich them with Linked Open Data. Focusing on education texts, it utilises XML/TEI formats to expand and visualise the enriched data. The project doesn't aim to cover every aspect of the final application, but presents a reusable workflow for future educational monographs. It strives to address the gap between scholarly editions and Semantic Web technologies, catering to readers seeking in-depth knowledge. Employing RDF modelling, it enriches TEI-encoded texts with WikiData and explores visualisation tools such as Mirador and IIIF. However, technical limitations exist, including incomplete data in WikiData and challenges in RDF implementation. The approach represents a significant step in leveraging Semantic Web for educational texts but faces practical implementation hurdles, highlighted by the need to address data gaps and integration issues between Semantic Web resources and academic texts. The advantages of such a tool for the reader-user would be the possibility of exploring knowledge in one way, to primarily browse the content of a text before even starting to study it. More than this, the digital text enriched in such a manner has the advantage to facilitate the learning process for all of those people struggling with learning.  

Moreover, we believe that the methodology proposed by this project might result in an advantage for both the publishing houses and the LOD world. The former could on one hand increase the richness of their data in the single text, on the other connect the complexity of this knowledge graphs in a common one, aiming to create internal (or collaborative) nanopublications[3] at the same time; the latter would see a new injection of data in their stream of knowledge, where the publishing houses would produce resources for those entities missing in the datasets such as WikiData but reported by the texts. An example of such a project is represented by the Edition Open Access[13], with many problematics to overcome[8].
